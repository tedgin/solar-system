The Solar System from Earth

This is a first-persion view of the Solar System from Earth displaying the Moon, the Sun, and the other seven planets. The Earth is looking directly at the Solar System's center of mass. This direction forms the ẑ axis. The horizontal or x̂ axis is aligned so that the span of x̂ and ẑ forms the ecliptic plane. The vertical or ŷ axis is aligned so that the Earth orbits the Sun counterclockwise from the perspective of an observer positioned on the positive ŷ axis looking towards the origin.

This simulation isn't real-time. It's not even accurate. It's an N-body gravitational simulation. The Sun starts out at the origin at rest. All planets start on the negative ẑ axis. Each planet starts with its distance from the Sun being the same as the semi-major axis of its real orbit. Each planet also starts with its speed set to the mean speed of its real orbit, moving orthogonal to its position vector so that it moves counterclockwise around the ŷ axis. Each planet's position vector starts out tilted towards the ŷ by an angle equal to its real orbit's inclination to the ecliptic plane. The Moon is similarly placed with respect to the Earth.
